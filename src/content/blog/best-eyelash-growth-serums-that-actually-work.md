Most lash [serum](/blog/best-vitamin-c-serum)s are glorified conditioners sold at serum prices. A few of them actually grow your lashes longer and denser, and those are the ones worth talking about. I tested 14 serums over several months to figure out which category each one falls into.

## What Actually Makes a Lash Serum Work?

This is where most [review](/blog/cerave-moisturizing-cream-review)s skip the science and go straight to "it made my lashes so fluffy!" — which tells you nothing useful.

Lash serums work through one of two mechanisms. The first is prostaglandin analogs, which extend the anagen (growth) phase of your lash cycle. The second is peptide-based formulas, which strengthen and condition existing lashes to reduce breakage and improve appearance. These are not the same thing. Prostaglandin analogs genuinely grow lashes — this is backed by clinical data. Peptide serums mostly make your current lashes look better.

Neither approach is wrong, but you should know which one you're buying. If you have naturally sparse, short lashes and want real density gains, you need a prostaglandin-based formula. If your lashes are fine but damaged from extensions or overuse of mascara, a conditioning peptide serum can legitimately help.

The catch: prostaglandin analogs carry real side effects — iris pigmentation changes (especially if you have light eyes), periorbital darkening, and mild irritation. They're effective precisely because they're pharmacologically active. Peptide serums are gentler but produce more modest results.

## The 14 Serums I Actually Tested

I wore each serum nightly for 8 to 12 weeks, applied to the upper lash line, using the same mascara for before/after comparisons. Here's where each one landed.

### Latisse (bimatoprost 0.03%) — $180 for 5ml, prescription only

This is the benchmark. Latisse is FDA-approved for lash growth and the only prescription option on this list. Bimatoprost is a prostaglandin analog originally developed for glaucoma — doctors noticed patients growing unusually long lashes as a side effect, and the rest is history.

Results at 8 weeks: noticeably longer lashes, modest density increase. At 12 weeks: the density gain was real and obvious. The downside is the price and the prescription requirement, plus the legitimate risk of iris color change if the formula contacts your iris directly. Application precision matters here.

Honestly, if you can get a prescription and your derm is comfortable prescribing it, Latisse produces the most reliable results of anything in this roundup. It's not a gimmick — it's a drug that does what it says.

### GrandeLASH-MD Lash Enhancing Serum — $72 for 3ml

This is the one everyone on TikTok has. It contains a prostaglandin-like compound (isopropyl cloprostenate) which is not FDA-approved for this use but functions similarly to bimatoprost. Results are real — I saw measurable improvement by week 10. The formula also includes [hyaluronic acid](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-benefits-skin) and vitamin E, which help with the conditioning side.

The controversy: isopropyl cloprostenate has been linked to the same side effects as prescription prostaglandin analogs (iris darkening, fat pad atrophy around the eyes with long-term use). GrandeLASH doesn't disclose this prominently. The results are legitimate, but if you're going to use a prostaglandin-adjacent ingredient, you should know you're using one.

At $72, it's significantly cheaper than Latisse. I think it's one of the better over-the-counter options if you go in with open eyes (literally and figuratively).

### RevitaLash Advanced — $150 for 3.5ml

RevitaLash uses a BioPeptin complex — peptides, biotin, and green tea extract — rather than prostaglandin analogs. This was originally developed by an ophthalmologist whose wife lost her lashes during chemotherapy. The formulation is genuinely thoughtful.

Results at 12 weeks: my lashes were noticeably stronger, less likely to break mid-shaft, and appeared longer because they stayed intact longer. The density gain was more subtle than GrandeLASH-MD — probably 15-20% vs. the 30-35% I'd estimate from the prostaglandin options.

The price is hard to justify for what is essentially a premium conditioning serum. But if you have light eyes and are concerned about iris pigmentation, RevitaLash is a effective peptide alternatives. No side effect profile to worry about.

### Vegamour GRO Lash Serum — $78 for 3ml

Vegamour markets itself heavily on being "clean" and vegan. The formula uses phyto-actives — mung bean and red clover — as alternatives to prostaglandins. Clean beauty credentials are fine, but I think the [clean beauty framework](/blog/azelaic-acid-benefits-uses) often prioritizes ingredient sourcing over ingredient efficacy.

Results at 12 weeks: marginal. My lashes looked slightly more conditioned, but nothing I'd pay $78 for repeatedly. If clean formulas are your priority and you understand you're trading efficacy for that, Vegamour is a reasonable choice. But don't buy it expecting Latisse-level growth because it will not deliver that.

### RapidLash Eyelash Enhancing Serum — $50 for 3ml

Good value pick. RapidLash uses hexatein-1 complex (peptides plus biotin and panthenol) without prostaglandin analogs. Results were consistent with other peptide serums in this price range — improved lash texture and reduced breakage, modest visible improvement.

At $50, it's accessible and the formula is solid. Not dramatic, but reliable. Good option if you're newer to lash serums and want to test the category without spending $150.

### The Ordinary Multi-Peptide Lash and Brow Serum — $18 for 5ml

I had to include this one. The Ordinary's formula uses a Biotinoyl Tripeptide-1 and Myristoyl Pentapeptide-17 combination. These peptides have decent evidence for hair follicle stimulation — nothing as strong as prostaglandins, but legitimate science behind the choice.

At $18, the expectations bar is calibrated differently. Results at 12 weeks: mild improvement, closer to what I'd expect from a conditioning treatment than a growth serum. The price makes it reasonable as a maintenance product or a starting point for someone skeptical about the category. I think The Ordinary consistently offers [better value than most people expect](/blog/cerave-vs-the-ordinary) in the skincare space, and this serum fits that pattern.

### Augustinus Bader The Eyebrow and Lash Serum — $145 for 6ml

Augustinus Bader's TFC8 technology (their patented growth factor blend) is genuinely interesting science — though most of the published research focuses on skin, not lashes specifically. The formula is elegant, absorption is immediate, and the applicator precision is good.

Honest assessment: I saw real improvement, comparable to mid-tier peptide serums. But I cannot confidently attribute results to the TFC8 vs. the other actives in the formula. At $145, you're also paying for brand positioning. If you're already invested in Augustinus Bader's skincare ecosystem, this is a nice add-on. As a standalone lash serum purchase, the price-to-result ratio is hard to defend.

### iS Clinical Lash Enhancement Serum — $100 for 5ml

A medical-grade brand with a well-formulated peptide complex. No prostaglandins, well-tolerated, and consistently well-reviewed in clinical settings. Results were on par with RevitaLash — strong conditioning, meaningful reduction in lash fallout, modest growth.

The $100 price point is justifiable if you have sensitive eyes or a history of irritation from other serums. The formulation is exceptionally gentle.

### Lancer Lash Serum Intense — $120 for 6ml

Dr. Harold Lancer is a respected Beverly Hills derm. The formula uses stem cell technology and peptides. Results: comparable to other high-end peptide serums, which means good but not dramatic. You're paying a premium for the brand and for Lancer's clinical reputation. Solid product, but not meaningfully differentiated from RevitaLash at $30 less.

### Shiseido Full Lash Serum — $65 for 6ml

This one surprised me. Shiseido uses their proprietary ReNeura Technology Plus alongside a peptide blend. Results at 10 weeks were better than the price suggested — denser-looking lashes and noticeably improved texture. Good value relative to the RevitaLash tier, and the Japanese cosmetics formulation standard is reliably high.

Honest recommendation: if you want a peptide serum with a strong track record and don't want to spend $150, Shiseido is my pick. The Vegamour money would be better spent here.

### NeuLash by Skin Research Laboratories — $150 for 6ml

Contains polypeptide-72 complex plus sodium hyaluronate. Results were strong for a peptide-only formula — one of the better non-prostaglandin options I tested. The application brush is finer than most, which helps with precision. At $150, it competes directly with RevitaLash and generally produces comparable results. Personal preference on formula texture would be the deciding factor here.

### Babe Lash Essential Serum — $80 for 2ml

Uses prostaglandin-adjacent compounds, similar to GrandeLASH-MD. Comparable results to GrandeLASH over 12 weeks — real growth, real density increase, with the same side effect caveats. At $80 for 2ml, the cost-per-ml is higher than GrandeLASH. Hard to recommend over GrandeLASH on value grounds unless you prefer the applicator.

### Talika Lipocils Expert — $65 for 10ml

French pharmacy brand with a long track record. Peptide-based, no prostaglandins, generous volume for the price. Results were comparable to RapidLash — respectable conditioning improvement without dramatic growth. The 10ml size makes it the best cost-per-use among the peptide serums I tested.
