If you've spent more than ten minutes on skincare forums, you've seen this debate. AHAs vs BHAs, glycolic vs salicylic, water-soluble vs oil-soluble. Let me cut through the noise and tell you what actually matters.

## The Core Difference

**AHAs (alpha hydroxy acids)** — glycolic, lactic, mandelic acid — are water-soluble. They work on the skin's surface, dissolving the glue holding dead skin cells together. Good for texture, brightness, and fine lines. Better suited to dry or normal skin.

**BHAs (beta hydroxy acids)** — primarily salicylic acid — are oil-soluble. This is the key distinction. Because salicylic acid is oil-soluble, it can penetrate into pores and dissolve the sebum and dead cells that cause blackheads and breakouts. Essential for oily, acne-prone skin.

That one structural difference explains most of the practical recommendations you'll see.

## AHAs: When They Make Sense

Glycolic acid has the smallest molecular size of the AHAs, which means it penetrates deepest and gives the strongest results. It's also the most irritating. If you have sensitive skin, start with lactic or mandelic acid — larger molecules, gentler effect.

The best AHA use cases:
- Rough texture or dull skin
- Hyperpigmentation and dark spots
- Fine lines (especially glycolic at higher concentrations)
- Dry skin that needs exfoliation without further oil-stripping

The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7% Toning Solution (~$13) is a solid starting point. Use it a few nights a week, not daily. Paula's Choice 8% AHA Gel (~$36) is a step up if you want something with more clinical backing.

## BHAs: When They're Non-Negotiable

If you have oily skin, blackheads, or active breakouts — BHA is your exfoliant. Full stop. Glycolic acid on acne-prone skin can improve texture but won't touch what's happening inside your pores.

Salicylic acid at 0.5-2% is the effective range for acne. The FDA allows up to 2% in over-the-counter products. Higher concentrations exist in professional peels.

Paula's Choice 2% BHA Liquid Exfoliant (~$34) remains the benchmark — tons of clinical research, consistent results, works across skin types. It's the one product I'd call genuinely proven for blackheads. CeraVe SA Cream (~$18) is the drugstore option if you want something more affordable, though the texture is more of a moisturizer-exfoliant hybrid.

## The Combination Approach

Nothing says you can only pick one. Many people with combination skin use a BHA on oily zones and an AHA for texture on other areas. You can also alternate — BHA Monday/Wednesday/Friday, AHA Tuesday/Thursday.

What you should not do is use both in the same routine, especially at high concentrations. That's a recipe for a damaged skin barrier. The 1-3 barrier is real — I've seen people obliterate their skin with the "more actives = better results" mindset.

One acid at a time. Give it 4-6 weeks before evaluating.

## What About PHAs?

PHAs (polyhydroxy acids) — gluconolactone, lactobionic acid — are basically gentler AHAs with a larger molecular structure. They exfoliate more slowly and are better for sensitive skin or rosacea. Less effective for serious concerns, but a good starting point if your skin reacts to everything.
