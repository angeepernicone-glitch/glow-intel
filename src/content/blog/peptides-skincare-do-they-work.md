Peptides are one of those ingredients that sounds impressive on paper and shows up on basically every [anti](/blog/do-collagen-supplements-work-skin)-aging product. But if you ask most people what peptides actually do, you get a blank stare. Let me give you the honest version.

## What Peptides Are (In Plain Language)

Peptides are short chains of amino acids — the building blocks of proteins. Your [skin](/blog/how-to-use-retinol-without-irritation) is mostly collagen and elastin, which are proteins. In theory, applying peptides topically signals your skin to produce more of those proteins.

That's the pitch. The reality is a bit more complicated.

## The Problem With "Signal" Claims

The main argument for peptides is that certain sequences — like copper peptides or signal peptides (Matrixyl, Argireline) — tell fibroblasts to ramp up collagen synthesis. The science on this is actually decent, not just marketing noise.

A 2009 study published in the *International Journal of Cosmetic Science* found that Matrixyl 3000 (palmitoyl tripeptide-1 and palmitoyl tetrapeptide-7) significantly increased collagen synthesis in cell studies. In-vivo results are harder to measure, but the mechanism makes sense.

The catch: concentration matters enormously. A product with 0.01% of a peptide in water and fragrance isn't doing much. You need enough of the active ingredient to actually reach the dermal-epidermal junction.

## Which Peptides Actually Have Evidence

**Matrixyl (palmitoyl pentapeptide-4, Matrixyl 3000)** — the most studied. Decent evidence for collagen stimulation. You'll find it in The Ordinary "Buffet" (~$15) and plenty of high-end serums.

**Copper peptides (GHK-Cu)** — among the most researched. Supports wound healing and collagen remodeling. Decent for post-procedure recovery or as an alternative to retinol for those who can't tolerate it. NIOD Copper Amino Isolate Serum (~$60) is the benchmark, though pricier than I'd like.

**Argireline (acetyl hexapeptide-3)** — marketed as "Botox in a bottle." It inhibits neurotransmitter release similarly to botulinum toxin, but topical penetration limits how much it can actually do. It's not useless, but the comparison to injectables is wildly overblown.

**Tripeptide-1, Tetrapeptide-7** — the Matrixyl 3000 combo. Good supporting data.

## What's Mostly Marketing

Anything labeled "biomimetic peptides" without specifying which ones. Brands lean on peptide complexity — "contains 15 different peptides!" — but more isn't better. It's a kitchen-sink approach that rarely outperforms targeted formulations.

Also worth noting: most luxury peptide serums charge $100+ for the same peptide concentrations you'll find in The Ordinary "Buffet" at $15. The markup is mostly packaging and brand positioning.

## How to Actually Use Peptides

Peptides work best as a leave-on serum or moisturizer, applied to clean skin before heavier creams. They play well with [hyaluronic acid](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-benefits-skin), niacinamide, and most other actives.

The one thing to watch: don't combine copper peptides with vitamin C or retinol in the same application. Copper peptides can destabilize vitamin C, and the combination with retinol causes unnecessary irritation for most skin types. Use them at different times of day.

## My Honest Take

Peptides are one of the few anti-aging ingredients where the underlying science is solid enough to justify using them. They're not as powerful as tretinoin (nothing topical is), and they're not as immediate as retinoids for visible results. But if you've maxed out the basics — SPF, niacinamide, retinol — adding a peptide serum is a reasonable next step.

The Ordinary "Buffet" is the obvious starting point. It's not sexy, but it works, and at $15 it's a no-brainer to try.

Just don't expect miracles. The "Botox alternative" positioning is garbage marketing. What you're getting is a solid collagen-supporting ingredient with a decent safety profile and real, if modest, results over time.
