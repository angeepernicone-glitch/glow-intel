Rhode launched in June 2022 with three products. By 2024, it was reportedly valued at over $1 billion. No traditional advertising. No massive wholesale retail footprint. Just a founder with 50 million Instagram followers and a product that was genuinely good.

But dismissing Rhode as "just a celebrity [brand](/blog/how-to-start-a-skincare-brand-2026)" misses most of what actually worked.

## The Product Was Legitimately Designed

The Peptide Lip Treatment is Rhode's hero product. At ~$16, it's accessible. The formula — containing peptides, vitamin E, and various butters — is genuinely effective. The flavors are appealing without being gimmicky. It fits in your pocket.

This matters more than people acknowledge. Celebrity [brand](/blog/tiktok-beauty-brand-strategy)s fail all the time — Kylie Cosmetics went from a billion-dollar valuation to selling majority stake at a fraction of that price. The difference is usually whether the product holds up to scrutiny without the founder's name on it.

Rhode's products would sell reasonably well without Hailey Bieber attached. That's the foundation. Everything else builds on it.

## The Aesthetic Was Intentional, Not Accidental

Rhode's branding — the rounded rectangles, the soft neutral palette, the "glazed donut skin" language — was positioned to own a specific aesthetic moment at exactly the right time. "Glazed donut skin" became a cultural shorthand. It wasn't just a product benefit; it was a movement.

This is brand building at its best: you're not just selling a product, you're selling a look, a lifestyle, an identity that people want to be part of. The glazed aesthetic spread organically because it was visually distinct and highly shareable.

Content creators ran with it. Hailey ran with it. The algorithm ran with it. The product became the vehicle for a trend, not just a lip product.

## The DTC-First Strategy

Rhode launched exclusively DTC. No Sephora, no Ulta, no department stores at launch. This was strategic:

1. **Margins** — DTC captures 70-80% of revenue vs. 40-50% with retail wholesale
2. **Data** — direct relationships with customers, email lists, purchase data
3. **Control** — pricing, presentation, experience are all in-house
4. **Scarcity** — selling out creates urgency and press coverage

The scarcity component was real in the early months. Products would sell out within hours of restocking. Whether that was engineered or organic demand outstripping supply — it doesn't matter. The effect was the same: "I need to get this before it sells out" is a powerful purchase accelerator.

## What Actually Drove Sales

Instagram and TikTok were the main channels, obviously. But the mechanics were smarter than just "Hailey posted about it":

**The phone case collab** — the Rhode phone case-lip balm hybrid got more organic press than a paid campaign ever would. It was genuinely clever. Every time someone held up their phone, they were showing the product.

**Micro-moment marketing** — Rhode seeded the right aesthetics at the right time. "Glazed skin," "clean girl," "quiet luxury" — Rhode products appeared in every piece of content about those trends because the brand DNA aligned perfectly.

**Selective partnerships** — Rhode was careful about which influencers they worked with. The brand maintained aesthetic coherence even as it scaled creator partnerships.

## The Lessons for Smaller Brands

Most beauty entrepreneurs take the wrong lesson from Rhode: "I need a celebrity founder." You don't. You need the underlying elements:

1. A product that genuinely works at a price that makes sense
2. A clear, ownable aesthetic that photographs well
3. A cultural positioning that taps into an existing desire or trend
4. Scarcity mechanics, even manufactured ones — limited drops, waitlists
5. DTC first, retail as validation later

The celebrity founder compresses the timeline dramatically. But the playbook works without one — it just takes longer.

The brands that are winning right now at smaller scale are doing exactly this: nailing the product, nailing the visual language, building a community on TikTok before they have a big marketing budget.

## What's Next for Rhode

The 2024 Sephora launch marked the shift from DTC-only to omnichannel. That's the typical scaling play: prove demand direct-to-consumer, then use retail for broader distribution and legitimacy signaling. The risk is losing some of the brand's exclusivity feeling. The reward is reaching customers who buy from beauty counters and don't scroll TikTok.

Rhode is navigating this well so far, but it's the moment where a lot of DTC [beauty brands](/blog/beauty-influencer-marketing-guide) stumble. Retail margins require higher volumes to compensate, and the operational complexity increases significantly.

Watching how Rhode manages the brand's "special" feeling as it scales into mass retail will be one of the more interesting brand stories of the next few years.
