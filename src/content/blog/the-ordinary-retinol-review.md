The Ordinary's [retinol](/blog/tretinoin-vs-retinol) lineup is a searched, most debated, and most misunderstood ranges in affordable skincare. I'm going to break down every variant honestly — what the differences actually mean, who should use what, and whether any of it is worth your time.

## What You're Actually Buying With The Ordinary Retinol

The Ordinary sells retinol in squalane at three concentrations: 0.2%, 0.5%, and 1%. They also sell a granactive retinoid line (2% and 5% in emulsion, plus a 2% in squalane), and more recently launched a retinal 0.2% emulsion. These are not the same thing, and the naming is genuinely confusing — even for people who know [skincare](/blog/peptides-skincare-do-they-work).

Here's the hierarchy in terms of potency and conversion efficiency:

**Retinal (retinaldehyde) > Retinol > Granactive retinoid (hydroxypinacolone retinoate)**

Retinal converts to retinoic [acid](/blog/azelaic-acid-benefits-skin) in one step. Retinol takes two. Granactive retinoid is the mildest, least irritating, and also the least studied in terms of long-term efficacy compared to retinol. When people ask "are The Ordinary retinols any good," the honest answer is: it depends entirely on which product you're talking about.

## The Retinol in Squalane Range: 0.2%, 0.5%, 1%

These are the straightforward ones. Retinol in squalane, no complicated delivery systems, no proprietary blends. Squalane is a good carrier — it's non-comedogenic, stable, and adds a small amount of barrier support without being greasy.

**0.2% Retinol in Squalane** — £5.30 in the UK, around $7 USD. This is the entry point. If you've never used a retinoid before, this is where I'd start. Expect minimal results for the first two months — that's not a knock on the product, that's just how retinol works. Your skin needs time to upregulate retinoid receptors before you see real turnover improvement.

**0.5% Retinol in Squalane** — £8.50 / $10 USD. The sweet spot for most people who've been using retinol for 3-6 months without major irritation. Reddit threads on this one tend to be positive, and I think the consensus is right. This is probably where most people will stay long-term unless they have specific goals.

**1% Retinol in Squalane** — £11.80 / $13 USD. This is the highest OTC retinol concentration The Ordinary sells. Genuinely effective, but also genuinely irritating if you rush into it. I've seen people jump straight to 1% and then blame retinol for destroying their skin. They should've started at 0.2%.

The squalane base helps with tolerability, but don't think it makes 1% retinol somehow gentle. It doesn't. You still need to buffer it (apply over moisturizer), start slow, and give your skin months to adapt. For a full guide on that process, [how to use retinol without irritation](/blog/how-to-use-retinol-without-irritation) covers the practical protocol.

## Granactive Retinoid: The Overhyped "Gentle" Option

Granactive retinoid (HPR) is marketed as a gentler, more modern retinoid. That's partly true — it's less irritating than retinol at comparable concentrations. The catch is that "less irritating" often correlates with "slower results." The clinical data on HPR is thinner than the data on retinol, which has decades of peer-reviewed research behind it.

The Ordinary sells this in two formats:

- **2% in Emulsion** — a water-based formula, slightly more moisturizing feel
- **5% in Bidens Pilosa** — the higher concentration, but Bidens Pilosa is an extract that's supposed to enhance efficacy (the evidence on this is limited)
- **2% in Squalane** — oil-based, similar logic to the retinol versions

I honestly think the granactive retinoid range is best suited for people who've tried retinol and found even low concentrations too reactive. Sensitive skin, rosacea-prone skin, or anyone who just can't tolerate standard retinol. For most people with normal to oily skin who can handle some initial adjustment, retinol is the better long-term investment.

## The Retinal 0.2% Emulsion: Actually Worth Paying Attention To

This is the most interesting product in the lineup, and it's genuinely underrated. Retinaldehyde is one step closer to retinoic acid than retinol — so theoretically, you get faster results with comparable (or slightly higher) irritation risk versus retinol. There's solid published research on retinaldehyde showing it's effective for photoaging with a good tolerability profile.

At 0.2%, The Ordinary's retinal emulsion is priced around £19.90 / $22 USD. That's higher than their retinol range, but still very affordable compared to brands like Avene or Paula's Choice that use retinaldehyde.

Who should use this? People who've been on retinol for at least 6-12 months and want to step up without going prescription. It's not a replacement for tretinoin — that's still the gold standard and still requires a prescription in most countries — but if you're not there yet or want to try something mid-tier, this is a legitimate option.

## Are The Ordinary Retinols Actually Any Good?

Yes, with qualifications. The formulations are simple and effective — nothing fancy, no proprietary blends, just actives in stable carriers. For the price, you're not going to find better. The 0.5% retinol in squalane at under $10 performs comparably to products from brands charging $40-60 for similar concentrations.

The criticism I'd take seriously is around packaging. Retinol degrades when exposed to light and air. Most of The Ordinary's retinol range comes in opaque packaging, which helps — but the dropper system means every use exposes the product to some air. If you're someone who takes 3 months to finish a bottle, you're probably using oxidized retinol by the end. I'd decant into a smaller bottle or just commit to finishing it within 6-8 weeks.

The other honest limitation: The Ordinary is not a luxury experience. The texture on some of the emulsion variants is slightly tacky, and the squalane-based ones can feel oily if you layer too much. These are minor gripes, but worth knowing if you care about sensory experience.

## How Do These Compare to Prescription Tretinoin?

They don't — at least not in terms of raw efficacy. Tretinoin (all-trans retinoic acid) is the actual active form. Your skin doesn't need to convert it. It binds directly to retinoid receptors. The clinical literature on tretinoin is the most robust of any topical ingredient in skincare, and it costs about the same as retinol when you factor in how slowly you use it.

I've written before about how tretinoin is underrated — most derms don't push it enough, probably because there's no brand marketing behind generic tretinoin. It's the retinoid that actually has 40+ years of evidence behind it.

That said, not everyone can access tretinoin easily, and not everyone needs it. If your goals are mild texture improvement, some prevention of early lines, and general skin quality maintenance, The Ordinary's retinol range is completely adequate. If you want meaningful anti-aging results in your 30s and beyond, you should be pushing toward tretinoin through a derm.

## Who Should Use Which The Ordinary Retinol

Let me make this concrete:

**Never used a retinoid before:** Start with 0.2% or Granactive Retinoid 2% in Emulsion. Use twice a week for the first month, three times a week in month two, then every other night. Don't skip sunscreen.

**Used retinol for 3-6 months without issues:** Move to 0.5% retinol in squalane. This is the one with the best Reddit consensus and the one I'd point most people toward as a long-term option.

**On retinol for 6-12+ months, want to level up:** Consider the Retinal 0.2% Emulsion or push toward prescription tretinoin.

**Sensitive skin that reacts to everything:** Granactive Retinoid 2% in Squalane or Emulsion. Accept slower progress in exchange for tolerance.

**People who can't tolerate any retinoid:** [Hyaluronic acid](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-benefits-skin) and niacinamide will become your best friends. Focus on barrier repair first.

## Can You Use Copper Peptides With Retinol?

This comes up constantly, especially with The Ordinary's range since they also sell a popular copper peptide serum. The short answer is: use them at different times. Some research suggests copper peptides and retinoids may work antagonistically if applied simultaneously — the copper can theoretically interfere with retinol's mechanism. The practical solution is simple: retinol at night, copper peptides in the morning. I wouldn't overthink it beyond that.

## The Reddit vs. Dermatologist Divide

The ordinary retinol reviews on Reddit are positive and honest, which I respect. Real users, real skin, no brand deals. The common thread in positive reviews: people who started slow and stuck with it for at least 4-6 months. The negative reviews almost always follow the same pattern — someone started at 0.5% or 1%, used it three nights in a row, got red and peeling skin, and concluded The Ordinary retinol is bad. That's not a product failure. That's an approach failure.

Dermatologist opinions are more mixed. Some love The Ordinary for accessibility; others are skeptical of "active ingredients" at budget price points. I think the skepticism is often misplaced.
