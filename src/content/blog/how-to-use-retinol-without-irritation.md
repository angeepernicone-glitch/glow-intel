[Retinol](/blog/tretinoin-vs-retinol) has more peer-reviewed research behind it than almost any other skincare ingredient. It increases cell turnover, stimulates collagen production, reduces hyperpigmentation, and is one of the few ingredients with solid evidence for both acne treatment and anti-aging.

It also makes a lot of people quit after two weeks because their [skin](/blog/retinol-purging-vs-breakout-difference) peels, turns red, and looks worse than before.

The problem isn't retinol. The problem is how people introduce it.

## What retinol actually does

Retinol is a vitamin A derivative. When applied topically, it converts in the skin to retinoic [acid](/blog/azelaic-acid-benefits-skin), which binds to retinoic acid receptors and increases cell turnover — the speed at which your skin sheds old cells and produces new ones.

This process is what creates the visible results: smoother texture, more even tone, reduced fine lines over time, clearer pores. It's also what creates the adjustment period. Faster cell turnover means more shedding, temporarily compromised barrier, and potential purging as clogged pores come to the surface faster than usual.

That adjustment period — the "retinization" phase — typically lasts 4-8 weeks. Most people quit during it.

## The retinoid hierarchy (what you're actually buying)

Not all retinoids are the same strength. From weakest to strongest:

**Retinyl esters** (retinyl palmitate, retinyl acetate) — Found in many drugstore products. Convert to retinol first, then to retinoic acid. Very mild, takes time. Good for complete beginners with sensitive skin.

**Retinol** — The OTC standard. Converts to retinoic acid in skin. Effective with consistent use. This is what most serums labeled "retinol" contain.

**Retinaldehyde (retinal)** — One conversion step closer to retinoic acid than retinol. More effective at lower concentrations, slightly more irritating. Underrated.

**Tretinoin (retinoic acid)** — Prescription-only in most countries. Works directly — no conversion needed. Significantly more effective and faster-acting than OTC retinol. The gold standard for both acne and anti-aging.

If you're using OTC retinol and not seeing results, you may just need a stronger form. Tretinoin isn't as scary as it sounds — it just requires getting comfortable with a longer adjustment period and, depending on where you live, a prescription.

## How to introduce retinol without destroying your skin

### Start low

Begin at 0.025% or 0.05% retinol. The Ordinary Retinol 0.2% in Squalane (around $8) is a reasonable starting point. Going straight to 1% because you want faster results is how you end up with a compromised skin barrier and a product you abandon.

### Frequency before concentration

Start two nights per week. Not every night, not every other night — two nights per week for the first month. Once your skin shows no irritation (no persistent redness, no significant peeling), move to three nights per week. Then every other night. Then nightly if tolerated.

This is slower than most people want. It's also how you actually get through the adjustment period without quitting.

### Buffering

If you're particularly sensitive, apply your moisturizer first, let it absorb for a few minutes, then apply retinol on top. This slows absorption and reduces irritation. Once your skin is adapted, you can switch to applying retinol to clean, dry skin for more direct absorption.

### Don't layer with irritating actives

While introducing retinol, stop using AHAs, BHAs, [vitamin C](/blog/niacinamide-vs-vitamin-c-which-goes-first), and benzoyl peroxide on the same nights. Your skin's tolerance has a limit. Too many actives at once compounds irritation and makes it impossible to know what's causing problems.

Once your skin is adapted to retinol (typically 2-3 months in), you can start reintroducing other actives on alternating days.

### Moisturize more than usual

During the adjustment period, use a heavier moisturizer than you normally would. Your skin barrier is temporarily more permeable than usual. [CeraVe Moisturizing Cream](/blog/cerave-vs-the-ordinary) applied generously after retinol helps significantly.

## The purging question

Purging is real. When you start retinol, cell turnover speeds up — which means clogged pores that were forming under the surface come out faster. This can look like a sudden wave of small pimples or bumps in areas where you're prone to breakouts.

Purging typically appears within the first 2-4 weeks and resolves within 6-8 weeks as the cycle stabilizes.

The difference between purging and a bad reaction: purging happens where you'd normally break out, resolves on its own, and improves over time. A bad reaction causes persistent irritation, redness, or breakouts in areas you don't normally get them. If it's the latter, you might be sensitive to the vehicle (squalane, the inactive ingredient base) rather than the retinol itself — try a different formula.

## Realistic timeline

**Weeks 1-4:** Potential purging and adjustment. Skin may look worse. This is normal. Don't quit.

**Months 1-3:** Texture begins to improve. Skin looks smoother. Tone starts to even out.

**Months 3-6:** Visible changes in fine lines and overall skin quality.

**Month 6+:** Full benefits becoming apparent. This is when before/after photos actually start to look different.

Retinol is not a quick fix. It's a long-term investment with solid evidence behind it. The people who stick through the adjustment period consistently report it as one of the most significant upgrades to their skin.

## A note on tretinoin

If you're serious about anti-aging or treating moderate acne and you're not using prescription tretinoin, you're leaving the most effective retinoid on the table.

OTC retinol works — but tretinoin works faster and more effectively at lower concentrations. The adjustment period is harder, but the ceiling is much higher.

In the US, services like Curology or Apostrophe can get you a prescription without an in-person derm visit for around $20-30/month. In many other countries, tretinoin is available over the counter at pharmacies at low cost.

Start with OTC retinol if the idea of prescription skincare feels like too much. But if you've been using retinol for 6+ months and want more — that's where to look next. And if you're new to skincare entirely, get the [basics down first](/blog/skincare-routine-for-beginners) before jumping into retinoids.
