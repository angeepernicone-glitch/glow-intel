This is a question that generates a surprising amount of debate. The "sunscreen every day no exceptions" camp vs. "you're inside all day" camp. Let me give you the actual answer, which is more nuanced than either side usually admits.

## The UV Basics

Sunlight contains two relevant UV wavelengths:

**UVB (280-315nm)**: The "burn" rays. These cause sunburns and skin cancer. Glass blocks UVB almost entirely. If you're sitting inside behind a window and not in direct sunlight, UVB is not reaching your skin.

**UVA (315-400nm)**: The "aging" rays. These penetrate deeper into skin and cause DNA damage, photoaging, and hyperpigmentation. Standard glass only blocks about 50% of UVA. Car windshields are specially treated to block more, but side windows and home windows are not.

So: if you sit next to a window for extended periods, UVA is a legitimate concern.

## When Indoor SPF Makes Sense

**Near windows**: If you work within a few feet of a window that receives direct or even indirect sunlight, UVA exposure over the course of a year adds up. This is especially relevant if you're treating hyperpigmentation — UVA will actively counter your progress.

**Driving**: Car side windows block almost no UVA. People who drive a lot often show more aging on the left side of their face (in countries that drive on the right). This is well-documented. SPF while driving is genuinely useful.

**Glass-heavy offices or homes**: Floor-to-ceiling windows, conservatories, studio apartments with large southern-facing windows — the UVA exposure is real.

## When Indoor SPF Is Overkill

**Deep interior spaces**: If you're 10+ feet from any window, the UVA reaching you is negligible. Applying SPF to sit in a conference room or a basement office is not doing anything meaningful.

**On cloudy days away from windows**: Clouds do reduce UVA (by about 20-30%), and combined with being away from windows, SPF becomes unnecessary.

## What About Blue Light?

Blue light from screens (HEV light, 415-455nm) has been a major marketing talking point for "indoor SPF" products. The honest answer: the evidence for blue light causing skin damage from screen exposure is weak. The intensity from a phone or laptop screen is orders of magnitude lower than sun exposure.

Studies showing blue light skin effects used light intensities equivalent to prolonged direct sun, not typical screen use. The specific "HEV protection" SPF products are addressing a risk that's largely theoretical at normal screen-use levels.

## The Actual Rule

SPF every day if you go outside, even briefly — this is non-negotiable. UV exposure accumulates even on cloudy days, even on short walks to your car.

SPF on days you're genuinely inside with no window exposure: probably unnecessary. SPF if you're working near a window or driving significant time: reasonable, yes.

The blanket "SPF indoors no exceptions" advice is technically cautious but practically overstated for most people. The "I'm inside, I don't need SPF" assumption is potentially wrong if you're near windows.
