Most acne routines online have you buying 8 products, half of which are probably making things worse. The reality is you need five products — maybe fewer — and the right ones will do more than a 10-step routine full of fragrance-loaded filler.

Here's what actually works, and why.

## What Does Acne-Prone Skin Actually Need?

Acne-prone skin has a few consistent problems: excess sebum production, clogged pores, bacterial overgrowth (specifically *C. acnes*), and inflammation. Your routine needs to address those things. It doesn't need rose water, face mists, or a "brightening essence." It needs a BHA to clear pores, niacinamide to regulate oil and calm inflammation, a non-comedogenic moisturizer so your skin barrier isn't stripped raw, and SPF.

That's the skeleton. Everything else is optional.

The reason most people's acne routines fail isn't because they're missing some hero ingredient — it's because they're using too many products with too many actives, over-irritating their skin, and then wondering why they keep breaking out. [Irritated skin and disrupted barrier function can actually worsen acne](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34738083/), which is the exact opposite of what you're trying to do.

## The 5-Product Routine (Morning and Night)

Let me give you the actual structure before I break each step down.

**Morning:** Gentle cleanser → Niacinamide serum → Non-comedogenic moisturizer → SPF  
**Night:** Gentle cleanser → BHA exfoliant → Niacinamide serum (optional) → Non-comedogenic moisturizer

Five products. Some nights you skip the niacinamide. That's it.

## What Cleanser Should You Use for Acne-Prone Skin?

I've said it before and I'll say it again: stop using harsh cleansers on acne-prone skin. Foaming cleansers that strip everything off your face feel satisfying but they wreck your moisture barrier, and your skin responds by producing *more* oil to compensate. You don't want that.

**What I recommend:** CeraVe Foaming Facial Cleanser ($14-16) or La Roche-Posay Toleriane Purifying Foaming Cleanser ($18-20). Both are gentle enough to use twice daily, both are non-comedogenic, and neither contains fragrance or irritating alcohols.

If your skin leans more dry-but-acne-prone (yes, that combination exists), the CeraVe Hydrating Cleanser works better — it doesn't strip as aggressively.

Use your cleanser morning and night. Don't double-cleanse unless you're wearing heavy makeup or SPF that doesn't wash off easily. Keep it simple.

## Why BHA Is the Most Important Ingredient in This Routine

BHA — beta hydroxy acid, usually salicylic acid — is the workhorse of any acne routine. Unlike AHAs (glycolic, lactic), BHA is oil-soluble, which means it can actually penetrate inside the pore and dissolve the sebum and dead skin cells that cause blackheads and non-inflammatory acne. It also has anti-inflammatory properties, which matters when you're dealing with red, angry breakouts.

[Research consistently shows salicylic acid is effective for reducing both inflammatory and non-inflammatory acne lesions](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4505095/), and it's one of the few OTC actives that actually has the evidence to back up its reputation.

**What I recommend:** Paula's Choice BHA 2% Skin Perfecting Liquid Exfoliant ($34 for 4oz) is the gold standard here, honestly. It's well-formulated, the 2% concentration is effective without being brutal, and it's been around long enough that the results are well-documented. If that price point doesn't work, The Ordinary Salicylic Acid 2% Solution ($6-8) is a solid budget alternative — just know it has a slightly different texture and some people find it more drying.

**How to use it:** Night only, after cleansing, on dry skin. Apply with a cotton pad or your hands — doesn't matter — to areas where you break out. You do not need to use this every single night, especially when starting out. Begin with 2-3 nights per week and build up based on how your skin responds. If you're getting flaky or tight, you're using it too often.

## Why Niacinamide Belongs in an Acne Routine

Niacinamide is genuinely one of the most useful ingredients for acne-prone skin, and it works through multiple pathways at once. It regulates sebum production, reduces the appearance of enlarged pores, calms redness and inflammation, and helps fade post-acne dark spots — which, if you're dealing with acne and hyperpigmentation, matters a lot. If that second issue is a major concern for you, I'd also read up on [how to fade dark spots properly](/blog/how-to-fade-dark-spots-hyperpigmentation) because niacinamide alone will only get you so far.

The concentration sweet spot is 5-10%. Below 5% and you're not getting much effect. Above 10% and some people experience flushing or irritation without meaningful added benefit.

**What I recommend:** The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% ($6-7). I'll be direct: this is one of the best value products in skincare. The zinc component helps regulate sebum production further, and the price point makes it easy to commit to using it consistently. If you want something slightly more elegant in texture, Minimalist Niacinamide 10% ($10-12) is good. Both work.

**How to use it:** Morning and/or night, after any actives and before moisturizer. I think using it morning and night is fine and often beneficial — niacinamide is not an irritating ingredient for most people at standard concentrations.

One note: the old concern about niacinamide and vitamin C interacting badly has largely been debunked. If you're using a vitamin C serum (which I'll get to), you can use them in the same routine without worrying.

## Do You Actually Need Moisturizer if Your Skin Is Oily?

Yes. Full stop. This is a persistent myths in skincare and it genuinely causes people to make their acne worse. Skipping moisturizer doesn't make your skin produce less oil — it just makes your skin dehydrated and irritated, which can worsen breakouts and make your skin overcompensate with even more sebum.

What you need is the *right* moisturizer. Non-comedogenic means the formula has been tested to avoid blocking pores. It also means you want to avoid heavy occlusives like mineral oil, petrolatum in high concentrations, or coconut oil — all of which are notoriously pore-clogging for acne-prone skin.

**What I recommend:** CeraVe PM Facial Moisturizing Lotion ($18-20) for nighttime. It's lightweight, contains ceramides and niacinamide (which pairs well with your serum), and won't clog pores. For daytime, if you want something even lighter under SPF, La Roche-Posay Toleriane Double Repair Moisturizer ($22-24) is excellent.

You don't need a separate eye cream. The eye area is thinner and more sensitive, but for acne-prone skin the moisturizer you're already using is fine around the eyes. Save your money.

## SPF Is Non-Negotiable (Especially With Actives)

If you're using BHA regularly, your skin is more susceptible to UV damage. Beyond that, UV exposure worsens post-acne hyperpigmentation — those red or brown marks that stick around after a breakout will stay darker and longer if you're not protecting them. SPF isn't optional in this routine.

The problem is that most SPFs are heavy, greasy, and genuinely comedogenic — which is the last thing you want on acne-prone skin. This is why I consistently recommend European or Korean formulations over most US drugstore options. The filter technology is better, the textures are lighter, and they don't leave that awful white cast. I've covered this in detail in my breakdown of [the best sunscreens for oily skin](/blog/best-sunscreen-oily-skin).

**What I recommend:** La Roche-Posay Anthelios UVMune 400 SPF 50+ ($30-35) if you can access European formulations. For US-accessible options, EltaMD UV Clear SPF 46 ($39-42) is the standard recommendation among dermatologists and actually contains niacinamide, which makes it doubly useful for acne-prone skin. Budget option: Beauty of Joseon Relief Sun SPF 50+ ($12-14) — Korean formulation, incredibly light, slightly pricey to ship but worth it.

Morning only. Apply as the last step in your routine, after moisturizer. Reapply if you're outdoors for extended periods.

## What About Treating Active Spots?

The five-product routine handles prevention, barrier health, and daily treatment. For active spots — the inflamed, red kind — a targeted treatment on top makes sense. Two options worth considering:

**Benzoyl peroxide spot treatment (2.5-5%)**: Applied only to active spots, not the whole face. PanOxyl Acne Foaming Wash 4% or the Neutrogena On-The-Spot 2.5% gel. Benzoyl peroxide kills *C. acnes* bacteria effectively, but it bleaches fabric and can be irritating if overused. Spot use only.

**Azelaic acid**: If your skin is sensitive or you're dealing with both acne and redness (or rosacea overlap), [azelaic acid](/blog/azelaic-acid-benefits-uses) is worth knowing about. It's gentler than BHA, still anti-inflammatory, and helps with hyperpigmentation. The Ordinary Azelaic Acid Suspension 10% ($10-12) is accessible and well-priced.

You don't need both. Pick one based on your skin's sensitivity and how aggressive you want to be.
