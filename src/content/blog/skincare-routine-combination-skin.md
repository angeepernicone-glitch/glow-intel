Combination skin is the most common skin type and, ironically, one of the most poorly served by skincare marketing. Every product is designed for either [oily skin](/blog/skincare-routine-oily-skin) or dry skin. When you're both simultaneously, generic advice breaks down fast.

## What Combination Skin Actually Means

Classic combination [skin](/blog/skincare-routine-acne-prone-skin): oily T-zone (forehead, nose, chin) with normal to dry cheeks. The reason is straightforward — sebaceous glands are more densely distributed in the T-zone. More glands equals more oil production.

The mistake most people make is treating the whole face like one skin type. Applying heavy cream everywhere causes congestion in the T-zone. Using a mattifying [routine](/blog/skincare-routine-dry-skin) everywhere causes tightness and flaking on dry areas.

The solution isn't complicated, but it requires a slightly different approach to product application.

## The Cleanser Decision

For combination skin, a gentle non-stripping cleanser is the right call — not a foaming gel designed for oily skin. Stripping the T-zone with harsh cleansers causes a rebound: skin produces more oil to compensate for what was stripped.

**Best options**: CeraVe Hydrating Cleanser (~$15), Cetaphil Gentle Skin Cleanser (~$14), La Roche-Posay Toleriane Hydrating Gentle Cleanser (~$16). All three clean effectively without disrupting the skin's natural oil balance.

## The Moisturizer Problem

This is where combination skin routines get complicated. A heavy cream is wrong for T-zone areas but right for dry cheeks.

**Option 1 (Simple)**: Use a lightweight gel moisturizer everywhere. Accept that cheeks might need a slightly richer application than the T-zone.

**Best gel options**: Neutrogena Hydro Boost Water Gel (~$25), COSRX Oil-Free Ultra Moisturizing Lotion (~$22), Belif The True Cream Aqua Bomb (~$42).

**Option 2 (More effective, more effort)**: Multi-moisturize. Lightweight gel on T-zone, richer cream on cheeks. This sounds extra but once it's habit it takes 20 seconds.

For the cheeks: CeraVe Moisturizing Cream (~$18) or Cetaphil Moisturizing Cream (~$16).

## The Actives That Work for Both Zones

**Niacinamide** is the MVP active for combination skin. It regulates sebum production in oily areas (reducing that midday shine) while improving barrier function in dry areas. Use it everywhere, twice daily. The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% (~$7) is the standard recommendation.

**BHA (salicylic acid)**: For the T-zone specifically — great for clearing pores and managing blackheads. Apply a 2% BHA only to oily areas 2-3x per week. Paula's Choice 2% BHA Liquid Exfoliant (~$34) on a cotton pad swept across the T-zone.

**Retinol**: Use uniformly, apply slightly less on dry cheek areas if they're reacting. The T-zone tolerates retinol better than most other skin types.

## Toner: Mostly Skip It

Toners were designed for an era of harsh bar soaps that required pH correction. With modern gentle cleansers, they're largely unnecessary. If you want a hydrating prep step — a watery essence like COSRX Snail Mucin or Klairs Supple Preparation Toner makes more sense.

Avoid any toner with alcohol, witch hazel, or astringent ingredients — they strip the dry areas and cause rebound oiliness in the T-zone.

## The Complete Routine

**Morning**:
1. Gentle cleanser
2. Niacinamide serum (all over)
3. Lightweight moisturizer (all over or gel on T-zone, cream on cheeks)
4. SPF 30-50

**Evening (2-3x per week — exfoliation nights)**:
1. Gentle cleanser
2. BHA on T-zone only
3. Light moisturizer

**Evening (other nights — active nights)**:
1. Gentle cleanser
2. Niacinamide serum
3. Retinol (starting 2x/week)
4. Moisturizer (adjust richness by zone)

## The SPF Choice Matters

For combination skin, heavy SPFs are particularly likely to cause congestion in the T-zone. The Biore UV Aqua Rich SPF 50 (Japanese import, ~$12-15) is the gold standard for texture — absurdly light, no white cast, works under makeup. ISDIN Fusion Fluid SPF 50+ (~$55) is the premium local option. Both are genuinely invisible on skin.

## The Main Thing

Combination skin doesn't need two separate routines — it needs smart product selection and, occasionally, smart product placement. Use a mild cleanser, niacinamide everywhere, and adjust moisturizer weight by zone. Everything else is refinement.

The 10-product routines marketed for combination skin are mostly unnecessary. Four products applied intelligently consistently beats ten products applied uniformly.
