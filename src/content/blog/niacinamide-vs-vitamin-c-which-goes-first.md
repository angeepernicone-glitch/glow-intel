You have probably seen the warning: "Don't mix [niacinamide](/blog/skincare-routine-oily-skin) and vitamin C — they cancel each other out." It sounds convincing. It gets shared constantly. And it is, for the most part, not true.

Here is what is actually going on, and how to use both ingredients properly.

## Where the myth came from

The concern comes from older research showing that niacinamide and vitamin C can form a compound called niacin when combined. Niacin is associated with [skin](/blog/how-to-layer-skincare-actives-correctly) flushing — temporary redness and irritation.

The problem with this theory is the conditions required. The reaction happens at high temperatures (well above anything your skin or bathroom would ever reach) and needs significantly more time than you spend applying your [skincare](/blog/skincare-routine-acne-prone-skin). In practice, under normal use conditions, this reaction is not happening on your face.

The idea has stuck around because the skincare internet loves a rule, and this one sounds scientific enough that people repeat it without checking the source.

## What niacinamide actually does

Niacinamide (vitamin B3) is one of the most well-rounded ingredients in skincare. At concentrations of 5-10%, it has solid research behind it for:

- **Reducing redness and blotchiness** — particularly useful for rosacea-prone skin
- **Minimizing the appearance of pores** — not closing them (that is not how pores work), but making them look smaller
- **Evening skin tone** — it inhibits melanosome transfer, which is the process that creates dark spots
- **Strengthening the skin barrier** — it increases ceramide synthesis in the skin

It is also extremely well-tolerated. Unlike retinol or vitamin C, most skin types handle niacinamide well from day one, with no purging or adjustment period.

## What vitamin C actually does

Vitamin C (usually as L-ascorbic acid) is one of the most studied antioxidants in skincare. The research is solid:

- **Antioxidant protection** — neutralizes free radicals from UV and pollution
- **Brightening** — reduces pigmentation by inhibiting tyrosinase (the enzyme behind melanin production)
- **Collagen synthesis support** — vitamin C is a required cofactor for collagen production

The catch with vitamin C is stability. L-ascorbic acid oxidizes when exposed to light and air, which is why cheap vitamin C serums often turn orange and stop working. A good vitamin C product needs to come in opaque or air-limited packaging and have a low pH (around 3-3.5) to be effective.

This is where many people waste money — buying a vitamin C serum in a clear dropper bottle that has already oxidized before they finish it.

## So, which goes first?

The standard rule for layering is to go from [thinnest to thickest consistency](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night). In practice this usually means:

1. Cleanser
2. Toner (if you use one)
3. Vitamin C serum (typically has a thinner, more watery consistency)
4. Niacinamide (can be in a serum or moisturizer)
5. Moisturizer
6. SPF (morning only)

If both are in similar consistencies, apply vitamin C first and let it absorb for a minute or two before layering niacinamide.

Some people find that using them at different times of day is easier to manage: vitamin C in the morning (where its antioxidant properties pair well with SPF), niacinamide in the evening. This is not because they interact badly — it is just a convenient way to space them out if you find layering multiple serums annoying.

## The one thing that actually matters more than order

The effectiveness of vitamin C depends heavily on its concentration and formulation. A well-formulated 15-20% L-ascorbic acid serum will outperform a poorly made 20% one. Look for:

- Packaging that limits air and light exposure
- A pH between 2.5 and 3.5
- Freshness — if it smells off or looks orange, it has oxidized

The Ordinary's vitamin C line can be confusing because they offer many different vitamin C derivatives (ascorbyl glucoside, ethylated ascorbic acid, etc.) with different stabilities and efficacies. If you want the most researched and effective form, L-ascorbic acid is still the standard. SkinCeuticals C E Ferulic (around $190) is the gold standard but not necessary — Timeless 20% Vitamin C + E Ferulic (around $25) performs comparably in most user tests.

## The short version

You can use niacinamide and vitamin C together. The cancellation myth is based on outdated science and conditions that do not apply to normal skincare use. Apply thinner textures first, let them absorb briefly before layering, and prioritize using a well-formulated vitamin C over stressing about the order.
