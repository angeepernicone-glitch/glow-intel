You just started [retinol](/blog/how-to-use-retinol-without-irritation). Week two rolls around and your face looks worse than it did before you started. Before you throw the tube out, you need to figure out if this is purging or a genuine breakout — because the response to each is completely different.

## What Purging Actually Is

Purging happens when an active ingredient speeds up your skin's cell turnover. Retinol (and especially [tretinoin](/blog/tretinoin-vs-retinol)) dramatically accelerates how quickly skin cells move from deeper layers to the surface. Any congestion that was already forming under the skin — microcomedones, plugged follicles — gets pushed out faster than it normally would.

The result looks like a breakout, but it's actually existing gunk clearing faster. You're not getting new breakouts — you're accelerating the ones that were already there.

This is why purging is temporary. Once the backlog clears, the [skin](/blog/azelaic-acid-benefits-skin) should be cleaner and clearer than before.

## Purging: What to Look For

A purge typically:
- Appears within the first 2-6 weeks of starting a new retinoid
- Shows up in areas where you already break out (not new zones)
- Consists of whiteheads, blackheads, small bumps — not deep cystic lesions
- Subsides after 4-8 weeks of consistent use
- Is accompanied by dryness and flaking (normal with retinol introduction)

The key location marker is important. If you normally break out on your chin and forehead, and that's where the purge is happening — that's consistent with a retinol purge. If you're suddenly breaking out on your cheeks where you never had issues, that's a red flag.

## Breakout: When Something's Wrong

A genuine reaction or breakout from retinol looks different:
- Appears anywhere, including new areas
- Includes large, painful cysts — not the typical small comedones of purging
- Worsens progressively rather than peaking and subsiding
- Includes significant redness, burning, or swelling beyond normal adjustment irritation
- Doesn't improve after 8-10 weeks

This type of response usually means one of a few things: the concentration is too high for your current tolerance, you're using it too frequently, you're layering it with other actives that are compounding the irritation, or something in the formulation (fragrance, certain emollients) isn't agreeing with your skin.

## What to Do During a Purge

The right response to purging is counter-intuitive: keep going, but slow down if the irritation is severe.

If you're using retinol 3x per week and the purge is manageable — stick with it. If your skin is raw and inflamed, dial back to once or twice a week and sandwich it between moisturizer layers (moisturizer → retinol → moisturizer). This reduces penetration slightly while keeping the skin barrier intact.

Do not add more actives during a purge. No glycolic acid, no benzoyl peroxide, no vitamin C at the same time. Give your skin one job to do.

## What to Do If It's a Real Reaction

Stop the retinol for 1-2 weeks, let the skin calm down, then try reintroducing at a lower concentration or less frequently. If you were using 1% retinol, drop to 0.5% or 0.25%.

If the reaction is severe or doesn't resolve, see a dermatologist. There are people for whom retinol is genuinely not the right fit — usually sensitive or rosacea-prone skin types who do better with bakuchiol or azelaic acid as gentler alternatives.

## Tretinoin vs. Retinol: Does It Purge More?

Yes. Tretinoin (prescription retinoid) is significantly more potent than OTC retinol, so the purge tends to be more intense and can last longer — sometimes 3 months. This is the phase where most people quit.

The first two months on tretinoin are often the worst. The results at month 6+ are often the best skin of your life. It's a long game, and the purge phase is the price of entry. If you can push through it with proper moisturizing and low-frequency application, it's worth it.

## My Honest Advice

If you're three weeks in and questioning your decision — give it six weeks before drawing conclusions. Document your skin with photos at the same lighting, same angle, weekly. It's too easy to panic when you're looking at your face daily.

If you're past eight weeks and still getting new breakouts in new areas with no signs of improvement — that's when to reassess.

The purge is real. It's temporary. And what's on the other side is usually worth it.
